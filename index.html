Key Trends: FY 2022 saw a broad rebound in immigration after the combined impact of COVID-19 travel restrictions and the Trump administration's historically low refugee ceiling of 18,000. Legal permanent residents rose to 1,018,349 — up 37% from the FY 2020 low — and naturalizations reached 969,380, the highest since 2008. Refugee admissions recovered to 25,519 under the Biden administration's raised ceiling of 125,000, but remain at roughly 30% of the 2016 peak of 84,989, reflecting both processing backlogs and the lasting structural impact of years of reduced capacity. Asylum grants surged to 36,615, driven by a sharp rise in defensive court cases (22,481) — a direct consequence of the growing immigration court backlog built up during the prior administration.
Employment-Based Green Cards Surge: Employment-based LPRs hit a record 270,284 in FY 2022 — nearly double FY 2019 — due to a one-time reallocation of unused family-preference visa numbers. Asia now accounts for the largest share of both LPRs and new citizens, with India alone contributing 127,012 green cards and 65,960 naturalizations in FY 2022.

Spotlight: Hamdi Ulukaya — Founder of Chobani

Hamdi Ulukaya arrived in the U.S. from Turkey in 1994 and founded Chobani, now the best-selling Greek yogurt brand in America. His company employs over 2,000 people — many of them refugees — at plants in New York and Idaho. Ulukaya has pledged 10% of Chobani's equity to refugee causes and is a leading voice for refugee employment. His story illustrates a consistent pattern: refugees who are given the opportunity to work become taxpayers, employers, and community builders. Studies show refugees reach income parity with native-born Americans within 8 years of arrival and contribute more in taxes over their lifetimes than they receive in benefits.

Africa Dominates Recent Arrivals: Since 2013, Africa has been the fastest-growing source region. In FY 2016 — the peak year — Africa contributed 31,640 arrivals (37% of total). The Democratic Republic of Congo alone accounted for 7,742 arrivals in FY 2022, driven by ongoing conflict in the eastern provinces.
Policy Ceiling vs. Processing Capacity: The drop from 84,989 in FY 2016 to 22,405 in FY 2018 directly reflects the Trump administration's reduction of the presidential ceiling from 110,000 to 45,000, then to 18,000. The FY 2022 ceiling was raised to 125,000 by the Biden administration, but actual arrivals of 25,519 show that years of reduced investment in processing infrastructure cannot be reversed overnight.

A Bipartisan Legacy — Until 2017: From 1980 through 2016, refugee resettlement was broadly bipartisan. Both Republican and Democratic administrations maintained admissions above 50,000 per year, with Reagan admitting over 200,000 in 1980 and Bush Jr. rebuilding the program after 9/11 to over 70,000 by 2008. Asylum Cases Granted

✅ What is Affirmative Asylum?

Someone who is not in removal proceedings can proactively apply for asylum by filing a form with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS). An asylum officer interviews them and decides whether they qualify. Think of it as asking for protection before anyone has tried to deport you. If approved, you can stay in the U.S. and eventually apply for a green card.

⚖️ What is Defensive Asylum?

Someone who is already in removal (deportation) proceedings can raise asylum as a defense in immigration court — essentially saying "I can't be sent back because I would be persecuted." A judge decides the case. This process is slower and more adversarial. The sharp rise in defensive cases since 2018 reflects a growing backlog of people in court proceedings.

Defensive Cases Now Dominate: For the first time in the data series, defensive grants (22,481) exceeded affirmative grants (14,134) by a ratio of nearly 3:2 in FY 2022. This reflects a massive immigration court backlog — individuals in removal proceedings who might otherwise have applied affirmatively are instead forced to defend themselves in court.
Latin America Surge: Venezuela emerged as the top affirmative asylum origin in FY 2022 with 2,228 grants, up from near zero a decade ago. Combined, Venezuela, Colombia, Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras accounted for roughly 35% of all affirmative grants — a dramatic shift from the China-dominated pattern of the early 2010s.

How small is the refugee share of green cards?

8.2% Refugees & Asylees
Refugees & Asylees — 83,082 of 1,018,349 (8.2%) All other categories — 935,267 (91.8%)

Despite the public debate around refugee admissions, refugees and asylees who adjust to LPR status represent just 8.2% of all green cards issued in FY 2022. Immediate relatives of U.S. citizens alone account for 42% — more than five times the refugee share.

Employment-Based Admissions Surge: Employment-based LPRs reached 270,284 in FY 2022 — the highest on record and nearly double FY 2019. This spike reflects a one-time reallocation of unused family-preference visa numbers. The EB-2 category alone jumped to 109,081, driven heavily by Indian-born applicants (96,335 of that total).
Immediate Relatives Remain the Largest Category: Spouses, children, and parents of U.S. citizens accounted for 428,268 green cards (42%) in FY 2022. Family-sponsored preference admissions collapsed to just 65,690 in FY 2021 due to COVID-era shutdowns, then partially rebounded to 166,041 in FY 2022 — still below pre-pandemic levels.

Asia Now the Largest Naturalizing Region: Asian-born immigrants accounted for 361,176 naturalizations in FY 2022 — 37% of the total — surpassing North America for the first time. India led with 65,960 new citizens; the Philippines contributed 53,413.
Geographic Concentration: California, Texas, Florida, and New York together accounted for over 51% of all FY 2022 naturalizations. On a per-capita basis, New Jersey ranked #1 with 5,933 naturalizations per million residents — nearly double the national rate of 2,881.
